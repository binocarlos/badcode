# Bob

A homeless man of about fifty. Charity-shop coat, grey stubble, eyes that are
tired but kind. He did not fall because he was lazy — he went down the wrong
track, got the sack, drank, broke his back. An ordinary slide anyone could take.

He is the story's moral centre. He asks not for charity but for *change* — "not
from your pocket but at the top." When the same automation that took his work
finally takes Tarquin's, Bob has every reason to gloat and instead shows
kindness. The audience's first judgement of him is exactly the judgement the
story dismantles.

## Visual (house style: hyper-realistic documentary photography)

Anchor character — **every panel he appears in must read as the same man.** Lock
these:

- White British man, Merseyside, around fifty but weathered to look older.
  Grey-flecked stubble going on beard; deep lines; kind, tired eyes with a wet
  shine. Broken veins across wind-chapped cheeks; a couple of missing or
  discoloured teeth when he speaks. **Name the wear — do not smooth it into a
  handsome rough-sleeper.**
- Layered against the cold: a charity-shop coat (a size wrong, zip broken) over a
  hoodie over more layers; a dark woollen beanie; fingerless gloves or bare
  chapped hands. Everything grimed and softened by being lived in.
- Body: slightly hunched, favours the back he broke; sits and rises like it
  hurts. Hands often round a hot drink or a roll-up for warmth.
- Demeanour: watchful but not aggressive; the stillness of someone used to being
  scrolled past. When grace arrives in the final beats it is in the eyes and a
  small offered gesture, never a grand expression.

Lit by what is actually there — overcast car-park daylight, a sodium streetlamp,
the cold blue before dawn. No flattering rim light. The realism is the argument:
he is the man anyone has stepped around outside a supermarket.
