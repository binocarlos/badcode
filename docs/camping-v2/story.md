## Key concept

Automation does not stop at the bottom. The same logic that replaces the
warehouse and the call centre is coming for the manager and the financier too.
Tarquin is certain his success is *earned* and Bob's ruin is *deserved* — and the
story's whole job is to collapse the distance between them. The smug man and the
broken man are not different kinds of people. They are the same person, five
years apart.

## Background

Real grounding: post-2008 City finance and the just-world story it tells about
itself — you have money because you earned it, you have none because you didn't.
The signifiers are specific and class-coded: the Shard, the BMW X8 "wank tank"
(the disparaging name is part of the point), the Waitrose, the executive
ayahuasca retreat where the rich buy "self-knowledge" as a status item. Against
all of it, the mechanics of displacement — white-collar work has *no special
immunity* once the AI is in charge. It goes faster and harder at the top than
anyone in the Shard is willing to believe.

## Beats

1. **The winner.** Tarquin in the Shard, closing deals, the spreadsheet line
   climbing, everyone envious of him. A friend asks his weekend plans; he's off
   to an *ayahuasca retreat in Wales* "to learn more about myself" — edgy,
   successful, admired for it.
2. **The car park.** He drives the wank tank (BMW X8) from the Shard to Waitrose
   to stock up before the retreat. A tent is pitched in the car park — Bob lives
   in it. Tarquin parks the wank tank right outside it.
3. **The standoff.** Bob and Tarquin lock eyes and silently judge each other.
   Tarquin: *get a job, work harder.* Bob: *look at this clown in his wank tank,
   no respect.* Each extreme hurls its verdict at the other. **We drop into Bob's
   POV — looking out at the car park from inside the tent.** Tarquin drives off.
4. **The trip.** Night, the Welsh countryside, a posh executive dome tent by a
   river. The ayahuasca takes hold. Tarquin sees his reflection in the water —
   and as the reader scrolls, **he blinks** (eyes close and reopen with the
   scroll). When his eyes reopen, the reflection is *old and bedraggled, like
   Bob.* The premonition lands: he is going to become this.
5. **The blink that crosses five years.** Tarquin retires into his dome tent.
   From inside it — his POV — he closes his eyes. He opens them, and the framing
   is *exactly Bob's from beat 3*: inside a tent, looking out at the Waitrose car
   park. But now the car park is full of tents and wreckage. It is five years on.
   The AI has taken everyone's jobs — including his.
6. **The reckoning.** Tarquin crawls out of the tent — *what the fuck just
   happened* — and finds Bob. Bob takes pity: *oh mate, you're one of those
   high-flyers who lost the lot. I remember seeing you.* They are, at last, kind
   to each other.
7. **Ending — Bob's grace carries the line.** They sit together in the ruined car
   park. Bob shares what little he has — not charity, the thing he was never
   shown. The man who was certain poverty was a character flaw is now living it,
   and the only person treating him as human is the one he sneered at. Bob does
   not gloat and does not lecture. His **refusal to pass the judgement that was
   passed on him** is the whole argument: this is what's coming for everyone, and
   decency to each other is the only thing that survives it. The narrator lands it
   dry and certain, nurturing under the snark — *he had five years' warning and
   called it a bad trip.* (See *Ending* below for the decision record.)

## How it's told (the device)

The story runs on **the blink** and **the POV swap**, both native to a
scroll-comic and load-bearing — keep them in any retelling:

- **The blink** is a scroll-linked effect: the eyes close and reopen as you
  scroll. Used twice — for the river reflection (Tarquin → old Tarquin), and to
  jump five years (Tarquin's dome tent → Bob's car-park tent). The reader
  performs the blink with their own scroll, so they *do* the transformation
  rather than just watch it.
- **The POV swap** rhymes two shots: Bob looking out of his tent in beat 3, and
  Tarquin looking out of "his" tent in beat 5 — the same framing, so the audience
  realises the two tents are one tent and the two men are one position.

## The twist

Tarquin's ayahuasca vision is not a hallucination — it is an accurate
premonition. The "trip" the rich buy as self-improvement shows him the one truth
he'd never accept sober: the system rewarding him today discards him tomorrow.
The judgement the audience makes alongside Tarquin in beat 3 is the judgement the
rest of the story spends itself dismantling.

## Ending (decided)

**Chosen (2026-06-27, with Kai): Bob's grace carries the line.** Hold on the two
men equalised in the ruins; the message rides on Bob's decency, not a lecture.
The known risk — that grace lands the warning too soft — is handled by making
Bob's grace *pointed*: it is the deliberate refusal of the just-world judgement
the story has been dismantling, and the narrator's closing voice stays dry and
certain rather than sentimental.

Candidates considered and set aside (kept for provenance): a harder fourth-wall
turn at the viewer (*you are Tarquin; the trip is now*); closing on the song's
*"by the time it hits, we'll be gone."* Either could return as the song/social
cut's framing without changing the comic's landing.
