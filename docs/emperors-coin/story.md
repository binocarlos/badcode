## Key concept

The scarcity of *money* is a story we're told, not a law of physics. A central
bank can always weave more — and can pay old debts by weaving more still — so the
only real questions are **who the cloth is woven for** and **whether it's ever
made into anything real**. Hoarded on the rich (assets), it does nothing — it is
literally invisible. Put to work building real things (productivity), it is the
only thing that was ever real. *The cloth was always invisible.*

## Background (the economics, so the metaphor stays honest)

This story is the *Emperor's New Clothes* applied to money. The fable's own detail
maps perfectly: the swindlers' cloth is invisible "to anyone unfit for their post,
or hopelessly stupid" — which is exactly the spell of economics. Question the
value of money and you're branded an illiterate, so everyone pretends to see it.

What the metaphor must teach, encoded — never lectured:

- **Money is conjured.** Fiat, fractional reserve, quantitative easing: the
  central bank creates money from nothing. The loom weaves cloth from thin air.
- **The magic money tree is real.** A government that borrows in its own currency
  can service old debt by creating more — printing to repay by printing more. The
  loom never empties; the tailors simply weave more cloth to cover the last bolt.
  This is *the* point to land: sovereign debt is not household debt.
- **The real limit is productivity, not a money-pot** *(the Keynes flag — plant
  it even if lightly)*. "We can afford what we can create." How much money *should*
  exist is a function of how much real value the available hands and resources can
  make at a given moment. GDP is a blunt proxy. We don't actually know the right
  quantity — and by the measure that matters (real things built), we may have
  woven far too *little* into productive hands, not too much.
- **Hoarding stifles.** Money parked in the accounts of rich investment firms, or
  sunk into assets, isn't being *used*. Worse, fresh printing flows to
  asset-holders first, so asset prices inflate and the rich get richer for
  *holding*, not making. The Emperor piles every bolt of cloth onto himself and
  sits on it; it clothes nothing.
- **The lie is selective.** "There's no magic money tree," said to a nurse about
  her ward (Theresa May, 2017), in the same era trillions appeared for the banks
  (2008, 2020). The scarcity is applied to some and never to others.

## Cast

- [`characters/dawn.md`](./characters/dawn.md) — **Dawn**, the nurse: the child

- [`characters/the-emperor.md`](./characters/the-emperor.md) — **the Emperor**: a
  billionaire (read him as an asset-owning oligarch, not a central banker), vain,
  who must always appear the most magnificently clothed.
- **The Tailors** *(supporting — may get their own file)* — two swindler-
  financiers who run the loom: the central bank / QE made flesh. They weave from
  nothing and weave more to cover old debts, protected by the "only the fit can
  see it" spell.
- **The Court** — ministers, economists, pundits who privately see nothing and
  publicly praise the robes. The priesthood of the fiction.
- **The Crowd** — us, cheering the magnificent economy down the parade route.

## Beats

1. **The vain Emperor.** A billionaire who *is* the kingdom's pride — "look how
   strong we are" measured by how magnificently he's dressed. He must always wear
   more.
2. **The tailors and the loom.** Two swindlers offer the finest cloth ever made,
   woven from nothing. Its magic: only fools and the unfit can't see it. They
   begin weaving him a fortune.
3. **The old debt — and the trick.** The Emperor frets about cloth he already
   owes. The tailors laugh: *we simply weave more to pay it.* The loom never
   empties. (The magic money tree, shown not stated.)
4. **The hoard.** Every bolt goes onto the Emperor — piled, sat upon, made into
   nothing. The looms roar, but only for the man who already has the most. The
   kingdom drowns in cloth that clothes no one.
5. **"No magic money tree."** Dawn, on the ward, is told there's nothing to be
   woven for the sick — as, across town, endless thread appears for the Emperor.
   She can see the loom from where she's standing.
6. **The procession.** The Emperor parades his magnificent (invisible) fortune;
   the court and the crowd cheer the strong economy.
7. **The child speaks.** Dawn says it plainly: *the cloth was always invisible —
   there was never anything to see — and what little became real, you only ever
   wove for him. The loom could have clothed a ward. You chose him.*
8. **Ending A — the parade marches on.** The crowd hears her. They know it's
   true. And the Emperor lifts his chin and keeps walking, because too much now
   depends on the lie. The procession continues. The warning lands in the silence
   of the marching crowd — *humans, you can all see it; say it sooner.*

## The device (the reader is the child)

Everyone *in* the comic sees the Emperor draped in shimmering golden cloth — a
fortune of money. But a scroll mechanic **strips it for the reader**: to *you*, he
is bare; the cloth is empty air. You hold the secret the crowd won't say, the
whole way down, until Dawn finally says it aloud — so the reveal is recognition,
not information.

Optional motif: a **loom thread-count / money-supply number climbing as you
scroll** (the "lead with the coin" idea, demoted to texture) while the ward's
supplies dwindle in the same frames — asset inflation and austerity in one shot.

## The twist

*The cloth was always invisible.* Two meanings, and the story earns both: money's
value is a shared agreement with nothing underneath — and money hoarded, unused,
is literally nothing. It becomes real only when it is made into something. The
"unfit to see it" rule is simply how dissent is shamed into silence.
