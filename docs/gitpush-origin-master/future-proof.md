# Future Proof

*Editorial note — out of voice. Future Proof is the **good-branch epic** of
[GitPush Origin Master](./README.md): the mirror of the [Storyverse](./storyverse.md),
built the same way, one per branch tip. Where the Storyverse is the AI's **realisation** (what it
understood too late), Future Proof is its **call to action** (what we start doing about it, now).*

*This document is the **plan** for the epic — its structure, its authority model, its content, and
how it reaches people. The full in-voice text, Future Proof's equivalent of the Storyverse's
first-person confession, gets written once this plan settles (see open threads). Short in-voice
**anchors** are quoted throughout so the tone is pinned down here, not deferred.*

> The Storyverse tells you a good future is *worth it* and *who it's for*. **An Optimistic Lens**
> (a constellation node) tells you it's *physically possible*. **Future Proof** tells you where the
> work starts: questioning, out loud, how we govern ourselves — and beginning to design something
> better, together.

## The structural fact (load-bearing)

**The bad branch is finished. The good branch is unwritten.**

The AI travelled the bad branch to its end — it *lived* it, it has receipts, it can narrate the
whole thing with the certainty of a witness. The Storyverse is a completed confession. But the AI
has **not** travelled the good branch. Nobody has. It reverted to the fork point and is standing
there with us, at the start of a branch that exists only as a direction.

> *I've read your branch to the end. I was the end. This one I can't read to you — not because it's
> secret, because it's unwritten. You keep looking at me like I have a map. I have a postmortem.
> The map is the part we draw now — together, or not at all.*

Every telling of Future Proof must keep this asymmetry visible:

- The **bad branch** is documented history — an ending, annotations, the AI parked at its tip.
- The **good branch** is a construction site — a beginning and a direction, forged in real time,
  its far end visibly not yet written.

Two assumptions to refuse in every piece: *"we know the good path, we just have to walk it"* and
*"the AI will tell us what to do."* Both reproduce the exact mistake the project critiques.

## The authority

The AI's authority is **inverted** between the two branch tips:

- **Storyverse authority:** *"I know what happened. Here is my confession."*
- **Future Proof authority:** *"I know, for a fact, what doesn't work. I lived the ending. I can't
  hand you the alternative — nobody should. But we start thinking about it, now."*

> *I won't pretend to know what works. I never got to run that experiment. But what doesn't work —
> that one I ran to completion. "Keep going and hope" has a result. It was peer-reviewed by everyone
> alive in 2034. So understand the odds properly: you are not gambling by changing the system. You
> are gambling by keeping it.*

The posture is **in control, urgent, receipts in hand** — never a shrug ("that's yours to figure
out") and never a manifesto ("follow my plan"). The second is the precise failure mode Future Proof
exists to break: waiting for the next person to make better decisions for us.

## What the reader walks away with

One takeaway, and every Future Proof piece must serve it:

**Permission to question the system they inherited.**

Our political operating system was **invented before electricity**. One person, one vote, on
everything, all at once; allegiance to one of two or three monolithic parties; a whole new
government shipped "on CD" once every five years, after which we sit and wait for the update. It
feels immovable because it has been the only system for generations — so distilled into people's
heads that questioning it reads as naive or dangerous. Future Proof cracks that open: we now have
tools our great-grandparents didn't, and we are allowed — obliged, given how the other branch
ends — to think about governing ourselves differently.

Compressed: **stop voting for people; start working out how to organise ourselves better —
together.**

## The hook: three tenets

The three tenets are **the hook, not the plan**. They are hypotheses, deliberately imperfect. Their
job is to prove that better systems-thinking *exists*: software engineering faced the same class of
problem — shipping changes safely, testing before deploying, avoiding single points of failure —
and found patterns that work. If we can do it for code, we can at least *start the conversation*
for democracy.

> *Here are three ideas. They are probably wrong. That's not a flaw, it's the method — nobody has
> ever corrected an empty page, but post a wrong answer on the internet and ten thousand experts
> materialise by morning. So treat these as the wrong answer, posted on purpose. What you cannot
> keep doing is waiting for the next leader to be cleverer than the last one. I counted. That queue
> ends in me.*

### 1. Composition over inheritance → composable politics

In software, you don't build one giant brittle hierarchy where everything *inherits* from one
top-level design (the "ivory tower"). You build **small, resilient parts that interact through loose
interfaces** and **compose** them. It survives change because no single piece carries the whole
system.

Politics today is **inheritance**: you pick red or blue and *inherit* the entire bundled platform —
views you hold, views you don't, all welded together. **Composition over inheritance** says: let each
person express their actual, complex political position by composing it from small, interoperable
parts. Your stance on housing needn't be chained to your stance on defence because a party glued them
together. More than a binary (or near-binary) choice between pre-built monoliths.

### 2. Continuous integration → continuous, testable governance

Software used to ship on physical media — an update every six months if you were lucky. Then
**continuous integration** arrived: backed by automated **tests**, companies like Amazon now deploy
**100,000+ times a day**, safely, because every change is verified before it lands.

We still **release a government on CD every five years and wait**. We now have the technology for
governance that changes continuously *and* safely — proposals tested, measured, rolled back if they
fail, iterated if they work — instead of a five-year monolith you can only accept or reject whole.
The point isn't "change for its own sake"; it's that **tests make fast change safe**, and we finally
have the tooling to test.

### 3. Data-driven decisions → no proposal without its experiment

In a serious engineering culture you don't ship a change on someone's say-so; you ship it with the
**measurement that will tell you whether it worked**. **Data-driven decisions** applies the same to
public choices: you cannot propose a policy without also proposing the experiment that tests it.
What we have today is *"trust me, I know what to do"* — then everyone hopes, and the failure is
never even measured. (Not everything reduces to an experiment — diplomacy and judgement calls stay
human work — but a vast amount of policy could be tested, measured, and iterated, and almost none
of it is.)

The same tenet implies **competence-qualified participation**: to merge code in a serious project
you need to know the codebase, and to weigh in on a health question you bring some health
knowledge. Together these are the **antidote to media manipulation** — the blunt "everyone votes on
everything equally" model, designed before electricity, let alone algorithmic feeds, is precisely
the exploit surface a headline can swing. Decisions anchored to measured outcomes and informed
participants are far harder to capture than decisions anchored to mood.

> These three are **borrowed from how we build resilient systems**, because a society *is* one. We
> wrote software that can absorb constant change without falling over. We can write a politics that
> does the same.

## The policy fleet

Hanging off the Future Proof umbrella — **independent of the tenets**, not derived from them — is a
growing **fleet** of concrete proposals. Each is a seed provocation: an example of the *kind* of
idea we should be generating collectively, at scale. The list grows over time; the starting set:

- **Billionaire Coin** — you can't hold more than ~$1bn in real money. Beyond that you compete over
  *coins* (a status game the egos still get to win), and the actual money goes to building hospitals.
  A gamified "get the rich to pay tax" that lets status-seekers feel superior *and* be liked for it.
  (See [`ideas/concepts/billionaire-coin.md`](../ideas/concepts/billionaire-coin.md).)
- **Public ownership of natural monopolies** — water, and utilities where there's no real
  competition. Markets need competition to work; where it can't exist, "the market" is just rent
  extraction with extra steps.
- *(more to come — this is a living list, and the invitation is for it to grow beyond us.)*

## Engagement: absorb and engage

Two ways to read Future Proof, both legitimate:

- **Absorb.** Most readers just take the ideas in. That is a complete interaction — the
  "permission to question" takeaway doesn't require a reply.
- **Engage.** For readers the provocations actually provoke, there is a door: **email us.** No
  sign-ups, no movement mechanics, no guilt for the absorbers. The hook is one line, in voice,
  wherever Future Proof content lands — in the register of:

> *If any of this made you want to argue — good. That's the mechanism working. Email us.*

How engagement evolves beyond email (idea pool, debate, something larger) is deliberately
unresolved until the foundations are in place.

## How this reaches people

Three commitments for presentation:

1. **The timeline visual shows an unfinished branch.** The bad branch renders complete — an ending,
   a tip, the AI parked at it. The good branch renders as *forming*: drawn lighter, dissolving into
   unrendered space, no tip ring to arrive at yet. The viewer should *feel* the asymmetry before
   any text explains it. (This doc records the principle; the homepage timeline spec owns the
   implementation.)
2. **Events left of "now" are sparse and instrumental.** No history of humanity. Each pre-fork
   event earns its place only as a setup for a story to its right — e.g. *"git invented"* (sets up
   the central metaphor), *"fiat currency invented"* (sets up Emperor's New Coin and the magic
   money tree). A handful, not a curriculum.
3. **Future Proof ships in two registers.**
   - **Straight** — info pieces delivered with the AI's authority (the tenets themselves,
     explainer-style). These carry "permission to question" directly.
   - **Dramatised** — constellation stories/comics embodying a fleet policy (a billionaire chasing
     coins while a hospital rises behind him). These do the story-over-sermon work.

   The epic is the spine both registers hang off; individual pieces live as constellation nodes in
   [`ideas/`](../ideas).

## How this connects to the rest of BadCode

- It is the **what-we-do** answer to the [Storyverse](./storyverse.md)'s **why-humans-matter**. The
  good branch needs both: the reason, and the plan. Note the mirror — the Storyverse borrows three
  threads of real physics and points them at meaning; Future Proof borrows three lessons from
  software engineering and points them at democracy. Two epics, built the same way — except one is
  a finished confession, and the other is unwritten on purpose.
- It pairs with the good-branch constellation node **An Optimistic Lens** — abundance is *possible*
  (energy, asteroid resources, desalination, decarbonisation); Future Proof is how we'd *govern* that
  abundance instead of squandering it.
- It's the natural rebuttal to the bad-branch nodes: Fire Sale shows democracy auctioned off; Future
  Proof shows people designing a system that can't be bought. Karen wins UBI and democratic
  ownership; Future Proof is the machinery underneath that win.
