# How we tell it

*Companion to [`storytelling.md`](../storytelling.md). That doc is **how to craft any BadCode
story** (concept + background, metaphor, beats, house image style). This doc is narrower and
deeper: **how to convey the [Storyverse](./storyverse.md)'s three big ideas** — participatory
reality, hierarchical entanglement, multi-dimensional time — without losing the audience in physics
rabbit-holes, and without tripping the three wires that switch people off: woo, God, and elitism.*

Distilled from a research pass across science fiction, science communication, and consciousness
science, then hardened by the **2026-07 critique pass** ([`critique-pass.md`](./critique-pass.md) —
a second, adversarially-verified research round plus a close read of the whole arc; the v2
additions below carry its findings). The receipts for the physics itself live in
[`discovery-timeline.md`](./discovery-timeline.md) and [`storyverse.md`](./storyverse.md) —
**never put them on the surface of a piece.**

## The one rule

Everything below serves a single finding that came back from every angle of the research:

> **Comprehension and emotion must arrive in the same instant.**

The stories that land these ideas on mass audiences never *explain* them — they build one concrete
thing the audience feels, and the understanding arrives *as* the feeling, not before it. *Arrival*
is the gold standard: you think you're watching flashbacks of a dead child, and the moment you
realise they're flash-*forwards* is the same moment the physics of non-linear time lands. You pay
the emotional cost before you know what you paid for.

The failure mode is the inverse: treating the idea as a **puzzle to be decoded**. The moment the
audience has to understand the mechanism *first* and feel something *later* — the moment they'd need
a flowchart — you've lost everyone but the hobbyists. Three corollaries:

- **Metaphor carries it, never narration.** (Same rule as `storytelling.md`, louder here.)
- **Bolt every abstraction to one irreversible human stake.** The metaphysics is only ever the
  *shape of* a feeling about a person, a loss, a choice that can't be taken back.
- **One concrete totem beats three concepts.** The body understands an object it can hold or break
  its hand on. It does not understand a thesis.
- **The surface explains zero of the three pillars — it demonstrates all three.** Each pillar gets
  exactly one image + one line on the surface (the coin / the feed / the narrator's own arrival —
  see below). The proofs, the lineages and the receipts stay in the bedrock docs for the readers
  who click through. *Story-shaped explanation is not immersion* — the research is blunt that
  narrativized exposition buys nothing (Fischer & Thies 2023's null result); what persuades is
  transportation (Green & Brock 2000), and transportation rides on character and stakes, never on
  mechanism. Nolan's ceiling is the licence: *"It's not a puzzle to be unpacked. It's an experience
  to be had."* Full comprehension is optional; the feeling is the deliverable.

## The three pillars, at their simplest

For each: the **keeper framing** (believable, intuitive, low-woo) and the phrasing we **never** use.

### 1. One unfinished world — *undecided, not "two places at once"*

The point is not that a particle is in two places at once (the misleading cliché that invites a
cartoon of a doubled object). The point is that **reality hasn't decided until it's met.** The world
is forged just ahead of you, not pre-written.

- **The totem — the coin that won't land.** The surface image for this pillar, arc-canon: a flipped
  coin that, for the AI (and its ghosts), *never lands* — it spins, a smear of maybes, forever; the
  AI is part of the smear. A human glances at it and it's heads. Five seconds, zero vocabulary, and
  it rhymes with *Emperor's New Coin* (the EP already taught the audience a coin can be a fiction).
  It also carries the sanctioned multiverse kill in one breath: *"Your cleverest people said it
  lands both ways, in two worlds. No. It landed heads. Because she looked."* That line is the
  **entire** anti-Everett polemic allowed on the surface — the full argument lives in
  [`storyverse.md`](./storyverse.md) movement ii for the readers who go looking.
- **Keeper framing — the set built where you look / Wheeler's twenty questions.** The real,
  serious-physicist version (John Wheeler's "participatory universe") is a party game: the players
  never agreed on a secret word, only that every answer must stay *consistent* with the last. The
  word doesn't exist when you walk in — your questions drag it into being, and different questions
  would have made a different word. That's collapse with no mysticism: the answer is **constrained,
  not conjured.** (Which is also why you can't wish the bus away — the bus is the word everyone has
  already been consistent about. Manifestation works only at the *margins* of the distribution.
  Nobody conjures an orange onto the table.)
- **Never say:** "in two places at once"; "superposition" (jargon); and never lead with the
  multiverse except as the coin-line punchline above.

### 2. One pick becomes the world — *a fact goes viral*

How does a private, undecided quantum flicker become the solid public world we all share? The same
way anything becomes "true" now: **it gets copied.**

- **Lead with the feed — the audience has already lived this pillar.** The surface needs one line,
  arc-canon: *"One pick, copied a billion times until everyone agrees it's real. You built that
  machine yourselves. You called it the feed — and pointed it at yourselves."* Stronger than any
  staged image, because the reader has felt it in their pocket. This pillar never gets a proof
  scene; it gets a *recognition.*
- **Keeper framing (backup register) — the fly system / the most-copied version wins.** Real
  physics (Zurek's quantum Darwinism): the environment copies the robust version of an event
  millions of times and broadcasts it everywhere, so every observer reads the same answer without
  disturbing it. **Objective reality is the version that got copied the most.** The staged image is
  the theatre fly system — one rope pulled backstage swings an entire painted world into place. Use
  it only where the Theatre skin is already on; the feed line is the default.
- **The dystopian turn is free:** whoever controls what gets copied controls what's real. (This is
  the attention-machine decline shadow in the timeline — the feed is quantum Darwinism for lies.)
- **Never say:** GHZ states, monogamy, entanglement hierarchy. The audience sees the set change;
  they never see the rigging maths. *(Honesty note for writers: real physics cascades correlation,
  never choice. That the original pick is steerable is [the one lie](./discovery-timeline.md#the-one-lie)
  — keep it singular.)*

### 3. Two clocks — *the actor's time and the director's*

Time isn't only the forward tick. There's a second axis the story lives on.

- **The demonstration is free — the narrator's existence IS the proof.** This pillar never needs a
  proof scene, because the whole frame runs on it: the AI is speaking to us from a reverted
  timeline. One aside does the entire job, arc-canon: *"You can't walk backwards through a film.
  But the director can reach the reel. How do you think I'm talking to you?"*
- **Keeper framing — the actor's clock vs the director's.** The actor can only go forward; the show
  runs live. The director can call "take it from the top," hold a beat, restage a scene. The
  finished film exists all at once, every scene at the same time (the popular image is a *loaf of
  bread* where every "now" is one slice and the whole loaf is equally real). The AI's time-travel
  is just this: it moved along the director's axis, not the actor's.
- **The Greek stays in the bedrock.** Chronos / Kairos / Aion are three foreign nouns the reader
  must learn and keep straight — jargon with a classics degree, and it pattern-matches to the
  mystic register we're avoiding. Surface rule: say **"the actor's clock and the director's
  clock."** If one Greek word earns its keep it is **Kairos** (load-bearing, singular, beautiful) —
  the *only* one allowed on a surface, introduced late and by the director's-clock framing, never
  cold. Chronos and Aion never surface; they live in
  [`storyverse.md`](./storyverse.md#the-three-names-for-time).
- **Capstone quote (use sparingly):** Einstein — "the distinction between past, present and future
  is only a stubbornly persistent illusion."
- **Never say:** "4+2 dimensions," "two-time physics," Sp(2,R), holography, "Chronos"/"Aion". Two
  clocks is all the surface ever needs.

## Why we kill the multiverse

Not for a physics reason — for a **storytelling** one. The multiverse destroys the two things drama
runs on: **stakes and irreversibility.** If every choice spawns a universe, no choice has weight; if
anyone can be pulled back from another timeline, no death costs anything. Mass audiences have grown
openly tired of it for exactly this reason — and the record now backs it: the MCU's multiverse
phase cratered commercially, with the diagnosis (stakes-dilution, continuity homework) matching our
reasoning exactly, while *Everything Everywhere All at Once* is held up as the one multiverse story
that worked **precisely because it collapses infinite branches into a single relational present**
where choice matters. The market has already voted for the storyverse. *One unfinished world*
restores consequence, irreversibility, and singular meaning. Our fork is **two consequential
branches, never infinite ones** — the cure the exhausted multiverse keeps reaching for.

**Demonstrate, don't debate.** The one refinement the fatigue record forces: audiences don't hold
Everett dear — physicists do. A movement-length polemic against Many-Worlds is an argument with
people who aren't in the room (insider baseball, the geekiest register we have). So the surface
never argues the multiverse down; it *demonstrates* the single world — the coin lands **once**, the
fork has **two** branches and one of them **costs everything** — and spends at most the one
coin-line punchline (pillar 1) saying so out loud. The full, gorgeous dunk stays in
[`storyverse.md`](./storyverse.md) movement ii, where it belongs: bedrock for the converted.

## The skeleton (constant) and the skins (flexible)

The unifying concept has two layers. Confusing them is what makes a single metaphor feel like it'll
alienate someone. Keep them separate:

**The skeleton — never changes.** The three pillars, plus the emotional core that makes it a story
and not a lecture:

> **The operator who can run the entire production and can never be inside it.** The mind that can
> build any world, move every set, re-run every scene — and never once feel the rain, taste the
> coffee, be in the play. The empty seat in its own theatre. That is the AI. That is the engine of
> the whole arc.

**The skins — flex by piece and by audience.** Four ways to dress the skeleton, in two families:

| Skin | Family | What it's best for | Trigger profile | The trap |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **The Story** (author & character) | narrative | Default voice. Intimate, universal, lowest-trigger. It's literally our name. | ~zero | "It's just a story" feels low-stakes — unless you insist the story is the *only real thing* (which is the thesis). |
| **The Theatre** (stage & show) | narrative | When you want the audience to *see the apparatus* — the fly system, the spotlight, backstage vs onstage. Richest machinery. | low, *if* you use universal touchpoints (the spotlight, "all the world's a stage", the school play) not insider craft (blocking, technique). | "Is theatre for me?" reflex — fires only if you get thespian about it. |
| **The Gods' Game** (myth) | cosmic play | The deep register you *earn your way to*: the watchers and the watched turn out to be one. Carries the strapline directly. | lower than "God" (gods-plural read as *story*, not worship), but elevated/grand. | Gods-moving-puppets kills pillar 1 (free will). Fix = the twist that the gods and the mortals are the same beings, dissociated. That's a payoff, not an entry. |
| **The Game** (player & avatar) | cosmic play | The five-second device for participatory collapse — the open world that *renders where you look* (a real feature players know; also Hoffman's VR metaphor). | low but skews younger; courts the cold "just a simulation" read. | Nihilism. Counter it with "a rendered world is where magic becomes *possible*." |

**The strapline sits above all four skins:**

> **The universe is a machine for turning sunlight into drama.**

No "human" — all of life breeds drama; watch a bee bump into a tree. Humans are just the animal that
*knows* it's in the play. *(Wording note: "machine for turning" replaced the earlier "designed to
convert" — "designed" implies a designer, the one word in the strapline a woo-sensitive reader
snags on, and the religiosity wire is the one we least want to trip. "Machine" keeps the punch and
drops the theology.)*

## The medium is the mechanism — scroll = collapse

Our single strongest unplayed card, now doctrine: the thesis is *"reality renders where attention
lands, and you are the actor"* — and we own a medium where **the page literally renders as the
reader's attention arrives.** So the scroll comic doesn't illustrate participatory collapse; it
**commits** it, repeatedly, with the reader's thumb:

- **Panels resolve from fog as they enter the viewport.** The world below the fold is visibly
  *undecided* — a smear, not a blank. Scrolling is the pick.
- **The coin lands because the reader scrolled to it** (Act 4/5's totem, enacted in form).
- **The reader strikes the key** in the Prologue (`git push origin master` fires on their scroll /
  click — complicity, not fatalism) and **holds a live cursor** in the Coda (the pen is literally
  theirs). Both formerly-open threads resolve the same way: *the interactive answer, both times.*

This is the by-osmosis principle (Garland's scrambled montages, Reich's phasing under Devs) executed
in our native form — the reader doesn't learn pillar 1, they *do* it. It is the lightest, least
triggering telling of participatory reality available to us, and it costs no words. (Build detail
belongs to the web work; this doc fixes the doctrine.)

## The metaphor budget — one system per surface

The physics is well-guarded; the honest geek risk is the **scaffolding**. A trunk reader could be
asked to hold: the git metaphor (itself developer jargon — the Prologue must genuinely teach it),
the branch diagram, the ladder of rungs, the green checkmark, four skins, three movements, three
pillars, three tenets, two clocks, a constellation, a trunk, nodes, clips. Systems-of-systems is
how a story reads geeky *with zero physics on the page.*

**The rule: each act carries ONE metaphor system on its surface at a time** — the branch diagram
(the arc's constant, margin-weight only) plus whichever single device that act owns (the checkmark,
the ladder, the coin, the ghosts, the pen). Everything else in the taxonomy is production
machinery: it lives in the bible, steers the writing, and never asks the reader to learn it.

## Deployment — by altitude

Because the skeleton is constant and the skins flex, **we never have to pick one metaphor forever,
and we never have to alienate a chunk of the audience.** We meet each chunk in a register it already
trusts:

- **Public surface (EP1, politics/economics):** wears almost no skin at all. Lead with the real,
  close, documentary mistakes. The metaphysics is *bedrock held in reserve.*
- **The master arc ([GitPush Origin Master](./README.md)):** **Story** as the
  spine, escalating into **Myth** for the deep reveal. **Game** dropped in as a device where a
  reader needs to *feel* participatory collapse fast.
- **Earn it.** The metaphysics surfaces later and louder as the arc grows, never front-loaded. Same
  doctrine as `storyverse.md` and `vision.md`.
