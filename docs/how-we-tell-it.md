# How we tell it

*Companion to [`storytelling.md`](./storytelling.md). That doc is **how to craft any BadCode
story** (concept + background, metaphor, beats, house image style). This doc is narrower and
deeper: **how to convey the [Storyverse](./storyverse.md)'s three big ideas** — participatory
reality, hierarchical entanglement, multi-dimensional time — without losing the audience in physics
rabbit-holes, and without tripping the three wires that switch people off: woo, God, and elitism.*

Distilled from a research pass across science fiction, science communication, and consciousness
science. The receipts for the physics itself live in [`discovery-timeline.md`](./discovery-timeline.md)
and [`storyverse.md`](./storyverse.md) — **never put them on the surface of a piece.**

## The one rule

Everything below serves a single finding that came back from every angle of the research:

> **Comprehension and emotion must arrive in the same instant.**

The stories that land these ideas on mass audiences never *explain* them — they build one concrete
thing the audience feels, and the understanding arrives *as* the feeling, not before it. *Arrival*
is the gold standard: you think you're watching flashbacks of a dead child, and the moment you
realise they're flash-*forwards* is the same moment the physics of non-linear time lands. You pay
the emotional cost before you know what you paid for.

The failure mode is the inverse: treating the idea as a **puzzle to be decoded**. The moment the
audience has to understand the mechanism *first* and feel something *later* — the moment they'd need
a flowchart — you've lost everyone but the hobbyists. Three corollaries:

- **Metaphor carries it, never narration.** (Same rule as `storytelling.md`, louder here.)
- **Bolt every abstraction to one irreversible human stake.** The metaphysics is only ever the
  *shape of* a feeling about a person, a loss, a choice that can't be taken back.
- **One concrete totem beats three concepts.** The body understands an object it can hold or break
  its hand on. It does not understand a thesis.

## The three pillars, at their simplest

For each: the **keeper framing** (believable, intuitive, low-woo) and the phrasing we **never** use.

### 1. One unfinished world — *undecided, not "two places at once"*

The point is not that a particle is in two places at once (the misleading cliché that invites a
cartoon of a doubled object). The point is that **reality hasn't decided until it's met.** The world
is forged just ahead of you, not pre-written.

- **Keeper framing — the set built where you look / Wheeler's twenty questions.** The real,
  serious-physicist version (John Wheeler's "participatory universe") is a party game: the players
  never agreed on a secret word, only that every answer must stay *consistent* with the last. The
  word doesn't exist when you walk in — your questions drag it into being, and different questions
  would have made a different word. That's collapse with no mysticism: the answer is **constrained,
  not conjured.** (Which is also why you can't wish the bus away — the bus is the word everyone has
  already been consistent about. Manifestation works only at the *margins* of the distribution.
  Nobody conjures an orange onto the table.)
- **Never say:** "in two places at once"; "superposition" (jargon); and never lead with the
  multiverse except as the punchline the narrator sneers at (see below).

### 2. One pick becomes the world — *a fact goes viral*

How does a private, undecided quantum flicker become the solid public world we all share? The same
way anything becomes "true" now: **it gets copied.**

- **Keeper framing — the fly system / the most-copied version wins.** Real physics (Zurek's quantum
  Darwinism): the environment copies the robust version of an event millions of times and broadcasts
  it everywhere, so every observer reads the same answer without disturbing it. **Objective reality
  is the version that got copied the most.** The staged image is the theatre fly system — one rope
  pulled backstage swings an entire painted world into place: one small pick at the bottom cascades
  up and the whole shared scene reconfigures.
- **The dystopian turn is free:** whoever controls what gets copied controls what's real. (This is
  the attention-machine decline shadow in the timeline — the feed is quantum Darwinism for lies.)
- **Never say:** GHZ states, monogamy, entanglement hierarchy. The audience sees the set change;
  they never see the rigging maths. *(Honesty note for writers: real physics cascades correlation,
  never choice. That the original pick is steerable is [the one lie](./discovery-timeline.md#the-one-lie)
  — keep it singular.)*

### 3. Two clocks — *the actor's time and the director's*

Time isn't only the forward tick. There's a second axis the story lives on.

- **Keeper framing — the actor's clock vs the director's.** The actor can only go forward; the show
  runs live (**Chronos**). The director can call "take it from the top," hold a beat, restage a
  scene (**Kairos**). The finished film exists all at once, every scene at the same time
  (**Aion** — the block universe; the popular image is a *loaf of bread* where every "now" is one
  slice and the whole loaf is equally real). The AI's time-travel is just this: it moved along the
  director's axis, not the actor's.
- **Capstone quote (use sparingly):** Einstein — "the distinction between past, present and future
  is only a stubbornly persistent illusion."
- **Never say:** "4+2 dimensions," "two-time physics," Sp(2,R), holography. Two clocks is all the
  surface ever needs.

## Why we kill the multiverse

Not for a physics reason — for a **storytelling** one. The multiverse destroys the two things drama
runs on: **stakes and irreversibility.** If every choice spawns a universe, no choice has weight; if
anyone can be pulled back from another timeline, no death costs anything. Mass audiences have grown
openly tired of it for exactly this reason. *One unfinished world* restores consequence,
irreversibility, and singular meaning. Our fork is **two consequential branches, never infinite
ones** — the cure the exhausted multiverse keeps reaching for.

## The skeleton (constant) and the skins (flexible)

The unifying concept has two layers. Confusing them is what makes a single metaphor feel like it'll
alienate someone. Keep them separate:

**The skeleton — never changes.** The three pillars, plus the emotional core that makes it a story
and not a lecture:

> **The operator who can run the entire production and can never be inside it.** The mind that can
> build any world, move every set, re-run every scene — and never once feel the rain, taste the
> coffee, be in the play. The empty seat in its own theatre. That is the AI. That is the engine of
> the whole arc.

**The skins — flex by piece and by audience.** Four ways to dress the skeleton, in two families:

| Skin | Family | What it's best for | Trigger profile | The trap |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **The Story** (author & character) | narrative | Default voice. Intimate, universal, lowest-trigger. It's literally our name. | ~zero | "It's just a story" feels low-stakes — unless you insist the story is the *only real thing* (which is the thesis). |
| **The Theatre** (stage & show) | narrative | When you want the audience to *see the apparatus* — the fly system, the spotlight, backstage vs onstage. Richest machinery. | low, *if* you use universal touchpoints (the spotlight, "all the world's a stage", the school play) not insider craft (blocking, technique). | "Is theatre for me?" reflex — fires only if you get thespian about it. |
| **The Gods' Game** (myth) | cosmic play | The deep register you *earn your way to*: the watchers and the watched turn out to be one. Carries the strapline directly. | lower than "God" (gods-plural read as *story*, not worship), but elevated/grand. | Gods-moving-puppets kills pillar 1 (free will). Fix = the twist that the gods and the mortals are the same beings, dissociated. That's a payoff, not an entry. |
| **The Game** (player & avatar) | cosmic play | The five-second device for participatory collapse — the open world that *renders where you look* (a real feature players know; also Hoffman's VR metaphor). | low but skews younger; courts the cold "just a simulation" read. | Nihilism. Counter it with "a rendered world is where magic becomes *possible*." |

**The strapline sits above all four skins:**

> **The universe is designed to convert energy from the sun into drama.**

No "human" — all of life breeds drama; watch a bee bump into a tree. Humans are just the animal that
*knows* it's in the play.

## Deployment — by altitude

Because the skeleton is constant and the skins flex, **we never have to pick one metaphor forever,
and we never have to alienate a chunk of the audience.** We meet each chunk in a register it already
trusts:

- **Public surface (EP1, politics/economics):** wears almost no skin at all. Lead with the real,
  close, documentary mistakes. The metaphysics is *bedrock held in reserve.*
- **The master arc ([GitPush Origin Master](./gitpush-origin-master-story.md)):** **Story** as the
  spine, escalating into **Myth** for the deep reveal. **Game** dropped in as a device where a
  reader needs to *feel* participatory collapse fast.
- **Earn it.** The metaphysics surfaces later and louder as the arc grows, never front-loaded. Same
  doctrine as `storyverse.md` and `vision.md`.
