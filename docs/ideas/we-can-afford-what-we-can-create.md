# We Can Afford What We Can Create

Keynes, BBC radio, 1942: *"Assuredly we can afford this and much more. Anything we can
actually do we can afford. Once done, it is there. Nothing can take it from us. We are
immeasurably richer than our predecessors."*

Money is a fiction; productivity is reality. If we can physically do something — build
hospitals, grow food, house people — we can "afford" it. The phrase "we can't afford X" is
always a political choice, never a material limitation. As AI and robotics push
productivity toward infinity this becomes unavoidably obvious, and the inversion lands as
a joke: to avoid massive deflation, money supply has to chase productivity upward — **"the
government won't be able to print money fast enough."** The thing we're told is impossible
(money printing) becomes the only thing keeping the economy functioning. MMT is the sober
floor: a currency-issuing government can't run out of money; the real constraint is real
resources, and inflation is the signal you've exceeded them — not that you've "spent too
much."

This is explicitly the **solution companion** to [Magic Money Tree](../magic-money-tree/README.md):
Magic Money Tree shows the mechanism of artificial scarcity (THE PROBLEM); this shows the
possibility of genuine abundance (THE SOLUTION).

The narrative vehicle is not yet chosen. Six candidates from the source doc:

- **The Economist's Nightmare** — Dr. Deficit wakes in the abundance future; "But who pays?" / "What do you mean?"
- **The Walking Dialogue** — "Money is the map. Productivity is the territory. You're refusing to walk somewhere because the map says 'here be dragons.'"
- **The Time Capsule** — a letter from the future: "Dear 2024, you had everything you needed. You just couldn't imagine it." (Could be framed as Bad Code's actual message.)
- **The Job Interview** — interviewing for a job in the abundance future; the horror of freedom.
- **The Heist That Fails** — "We stole a billion pounds!" / "Cool. Here's two billion. They were about to recycle it anyway."
- **The Factory Floor** — the last human factory worker's job is deciding what to make next.

What it still needs: a protagonist with stakes (who's the Victoria Davey of this one?), a
concrete setting, and the twist — the "where does paper come from?" moment of this story.
