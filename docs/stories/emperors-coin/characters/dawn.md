# Dawn

A ward nurse, around her forties. Warm, competent, exhausted — the person who
holds a failing system together with her own hands and is told, again and again,
that there's simply no money for what the sick need.

She is the fable's child — the one who says the Emperor is wearing nothing — but
she earns it through an arc, not a gimmick:

1. **Complicit, like everyone.** Too tired to argue, she accepts "there's no magic
   money tree" because that's just how the world is. She claps for the procession
   like the rest of the crowd.
2. **The dissonance.** Told there's nothing for her ward in the same breath the
   looms roar endless thread for the Emperor, the lie stops fitting. She can *see*
   the loom from where she stands.
3. **Clarity, then voice.** She stops pretending. Plainly, without a speech, she
   says the cloth was always invisible — and that what little became real was only
   ever woven for him.

Per the ending, she does not "win" — the parade marches on. But she is the one who
stopped pretending, and the crowd heard her. She's sympathetic, never a mouthpiece;
the politics arrive through her exhaustion and her plain words, not a lecture.
