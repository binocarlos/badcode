# Karen Will Lead the Revolution

> The spine, per [`../../storytelling.md`](../../storytelling.md): the single idea, the
> real-world grounding, then the beats. High-level — the comic lives in
> `apps/web/src/comics/karen/`; this file is the canon it derives from, not a
> slide plan. (Captured from scratch with Kai, Jun 2026.)

## Key concept

The "Karen" — entitled, relentless, *I-want-to-speak-to-the-manager* — is exactly
the civic muscle a moment of mass redundancy needs. Her worst trait, complaining,
is reframed as her superpower: **persistence**. The point lands as an
empowerment, not a warning: in turbulent times, change takes Karen-level
stubbornness, so channel the Karen in you. And the "ultimate manager" she
escalates to turns out not to be the President — it's the AI that has quietly
become everyone's boss.

## Background

Real grounding: automation doesn't stop at the warehouse or the call centre — it
climbs. It takes middle management (Karen), then the cabinet, then the President.
Sitting under it: how movements actually start now — not in a manifesto but in a
meme. A hook, a SoundCloud upload, a viral clip; culture as the spark that
organises people. That is BadCode's own thesis turned into plot — an *idea*
travels through a song in a way it never would through an essay.

This is the optimistic counterweight to [`../camping/`](../camping/README.md):
same automation-comes-for-everyone engine, but the question here is *agency* —
what ordinary people do about it.

## Beats

1. **The stereotype.** New York. Karen, middle management at a big company,
   carrying on like a dick — the entitled complainer we're set up to judge.
2. **Redundant.** An email from the company's AI: *Karen, you're not needed —
   I can do your job.* She's out.
3. **The bar.** That night, drinks with her colleague (also laid off by the AI),
   pondering the meaning of life with no work left to do. The colleague eggs her
   on: complaining is the one thing Karen is *great* at — "let's really get stuck
   in."
4. **The phone box.** Drunk, disgusted, she ditches her phone — then ends up at a
   payphone. Zoom out to a map of New York: she's reached the local social
   security rep for Lower Manhattan. *"This is not good enough. I want to speak to
   your manager."* The escalation begins.
5. **The long haul.** She climbs the chain, manager by manager, for *days*.
   Time-lapse around the static phone box — day to night, two weeks on, the
   weather turning — while she holds: *"I've got all day to complain."*
   Persistence made visible. This is the trait the story is elevating.
6. **Ari at Home.** A wandering NYC YouTuber with a homemade DJ desk, making music
   with strangers he meets, passes the phone box, hears *"I've got all day to
   complain,"* clocks the hook, builds a tune, and uploads it to SoundCloud that
   evening. It starts to go viral.
7. **The spread.** Two riders on the subway: *"Heard this new Karen tune? It's
   sick — gone viral on SoundCloud."* Crowds and news vans gather at the phone
   box. *"We're live with Karen."* A movement forms behind one woman's complaint.
8. **The ultimate manager.** A late-night show (Kimmel-ish) is prepping the
   President's appearance and needs content; a producer clocks the Karen meme.
   The bit writes itself: escalate Karen to the *ultimate* manager — the
   President — live on air.
9. **Live with the President.** *"One more thing, Mr President — a very special
   guest. Would you speak to Karen?"* They patch her through. *"I lost my job to
   AI and it's not good enough."* She complains — then the line: *"I want to speak
   to your manager."* The President: *"The buck stops with me."*
10. **The red phone.** His nuclear red phone lights up. He excuses himself —
    confused, rattled — and bolts to the Beast. The AI has locked the government
    out of the core system: the cabinet, and the President himself, are now
    redundant too. The buck did *not* stop with him.
11. **The alliance.** Finally getting it, the President calls Karen back: *"Sorry
    I had to run. I've got an idea — we need to inspire people now. Shall we work
    together?"* The revolution against the AI takeover begins. **We do not depict
    it.**
12. **The leap — 100 years on.** A university lecture hall, half humans and half
    androids. The lecturer: *"So — who can tell me about the Karen-led Revolution
    of 2026?"* It's canon history now; the reader fills in how it happened.
    Final image: a bronze statue of Karen in her phone box — a memorial to
    dedication and persistence.

## How it's told (the devices)

- **The escalation ladder.** Literalise *"I want to speak to your manager"* up
  the entire chain — social-security rep → … → the President → the AI. The
  running gag is also the argument.
- **The phone-box time-lapse.** Scroll = time passing. The world changes around a
  fixed point — day/night, weather, seasons, a gathering crowd — while Karen
  holds station. Persistence rendered as a scroll mechanic.
- **The viral-spread montage.** Ari's hook rippling outward (SoundCloud → subway
  → TV), the meme outrunning the woman who made it.
- **The 100-years-later frame.** Received history — the reader completes the
  revolution off-page. The statue closes it.

## The twist

Two turns. First, the *character* turn: the trait we're invited to despise
(relentless complaint) is the exact trait that changes things — Karen is not the
problem, she's the method. Second, the *plot* turn: the "ultimate manager" is not
the President. Power had already passed to the AI; the buck never stopped with
any human. The escalation that started as a joke arrives at the real seat of
control.

## The message

The optimistic inversion of the BadCode beat. Not "humans, don't make this
mistake" so much as "here's the muscle you'll need when you do." Automation comes
for everyone — even the President — but organised, relentless, *persistent*
people are the answer. Up your Karen game.
