# Dawn

A nurse in her late fifties. She has kept strangers alive for thirty years and
has nothing to show for it but sore feet and a kindness she refuses to spend
down. Widowed. Her mother is in care she can barely fund. She is not a victim
and she is not a saint — she is the person the whole system quietly runs on and
loudly cannot afford.

The story turns the audience's first glance on its head: she is "just" a tired
old nurse, easy to scroll past, and by the end she is the one who did the
arithmetic the powerful hoped no one would.

## Visual (house style: hyper-realistic documentary photography)

Single anchor character — **every panel she appears in must read as the same
woman.** Lock these:

- Late-50s white British woman, Midlands. Short greying mid-brown hair, a few
  flyaways escaping a clip — no time for vanity. Kind, tired eyes with deep lines
  around them; pale, no makeup.
- Pale teal/NHS-blue scrubs, slightly worn and creased from a long shift. A
  lanyard with an ID badge; an upside-down **fob watch** pinned to her chest.
  Cheap digital wristwatch. Reading glasses pushed up on her head or on a cord.
- Plain gold wedding ring (widowed). Sensible black work shoes. Slightly stooped
  from decades on her feet; stands and sits like someone whose back aches.
- Demeanour: composed, gentle, contained exhaustion. Never mugging for the
  camera — caught mid-moment, as if an invisible lens witnessed her.

The realism is the point: she is ordinary and close, the nurse anyone has been
treated by, not a movie nurse.
