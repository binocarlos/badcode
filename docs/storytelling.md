# How we craft a story

We are not pretending to be master storytellers. We'd rather **stand on the shoulders of giants** —
study how great stories work and borrow their machinery — than wing it. This doc is the method.

> Companion: [`how-we-tell-it.md`](./how-we-tell-it.md) goes deeper on the one job this doc only
> gestures at — conveying the [Storyverse](./storyverse.md)'s three big ideas (participatory
> reality, hierarchical entanglement, multi-dimensional time) without losing the audience. The
> skeleton, the four skins, the simplest believable framing for each pillar.

## The spine of a BadCode story

Every story starts from two things written down explicitly:

1. **Key concept** — the single idea we want lodged in the audience's head (e.g. "the people who did
   everything right are the least prepared for automation").
2. **Background** — the real-world grounding that makes it land (the actual mechanics of the 2008
   crash, how benefits bots work, what quantitative easing is).

The craft is then to **work the key concept and background *into the metaphor*** — into the
characters, the beats, and the punchline — so the audience feels the point rather than being told it.

## Principles

- **Metaphor carries the message.** If the meaning lives only in narration, we've failed. It should
  be encoded in what happens.
- **Use twists sparingly and cleverly.** A good turn sharpens the point (Camping's "the dream was
  actually the future"); too many turns just confuse. Don't over-egg it.
- **Beats and meter matter.** Pay attention to structure, pacing, and rhythm — in both the story and
  the music. Research the mechanics; apply them deliberately.
- **Sympathy where the surface says otherwise.** The homeless man, the laid-off worker, the "Karen"
  — the story should turn the audience's first judgement on its head.
- **Land the warning.** Subtly or bluntly, end on "humans, please don't make this obvious mistake."

## Working process

1. Write the **concept + background** for the story.
2. Research storytelling techniques and comparable stories — find the machinery worth borrowing.
3. Draft **beats** (scene-by-scene), then **characters** (each visual/behavioural choice loaded with
   meaning), then **image/style direction**.
4. Develop in [`ideas/`](../ideas) using the templates; promote to a coded comic in `apps/web` when ready.

## Image direction (house style)

Default visual target is **hyper-realistic documentary photography** — as if an invisible camera
witnessed a real, unstaged moment (think a high-res RAW file: deep depth of field, situational
lighting, no lens flares, no bokeh tricks, no filters). Every character's clothing, grooming, and
posture is chosen to signal their worldview and class position. The realism is the point: these
mistakes are ordinary and close, not science fiction.
